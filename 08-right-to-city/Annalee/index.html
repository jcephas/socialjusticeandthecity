Social Justice and the City

Annalee Fuller | Prof. Jay Cephas

The Right to the City

Planning | Domains | Empowerment

In Writings on Cities, Henri Lefebvre reflects on trends of urbanization, the removal of anthropological needs from top-down planning schema, and re-imagines the relationship between humans and urbanity. He evaluates intellectual shifts through Nietzscheian- nihilist perspectives and envisions a new “urban man,” whose relationship with the city would be enriched through praxis. The needs of their body and intellect would be honored, even essential, to the prescription-- better, permission-- of social interactions in designs of the urban environment. Consumption, he argues, has stood in place as a driver of urban form, but consumption must not stand in for authentic, anthropological needs.

In Stuart Cowan’s “The City Repair Project,” he describes a participatory design project in which members of a community are empowered to contribute their creative ideas to the development of a neighborhood center in Portland. Its model offers significant contrast to definitive city plans conceived exclusively by city and state officials.

These readings highlight critiques to how power structures influence the domain which the general public is permitted to occupy. At left, a map (which reincorporates data from “Right to Landscape”) shows where designated Open Spaces in Boston overlap with areas increasingly prone to flood risk in coming decades. As we notice their intersections, we may begin to speculate about how long-term planning and outfitting the city for consequences of sea level rise may have influenced their decision to locate the open space where they did. Such ideas then surface concerns about displacement and debates over ethical responses to bracing for climate change in cities. An interesting next step for this map could be to highlight which spaces are adjacent to or inclusive of administrative buildings, “Urban Renewal” projects, and major retail thoroughfares. What open space, then, would be left?
