The Body & Landscape

In chapter 1 of The Right to Landscape, the authors cite various practitioners to assert that the right to landscape is inherently tied to basic human rights and overall well-being, while Cordingley provides a narrative case study of Can Masdeu to describe the ways in which they have defended their landscape through community and activism.

Through their attempt to define the term “landscape”, Egoz, Makhzoumi, and Pungetti stake the amorphous and multifaceted definitions of a landscape that lend to its power. They additionally touch upon themes that have come up through previous readings. The concept that land equals power relates to the politics that surround this notion of using landscape to promote social justice. One interesting idea that they cite is the temporal aspect of landscapes, as they can be subjected to a specific time and place. These concepts are similar to the ways in which we have understood technologies of the body. They state, “landscape therefore is simultaneously a product such as arable field, pasture land, settlement and garden, and the act of production embedding intent, design and action.” [2] Here we can understand how landscape can be both a subject and subjected by its surroundings, similarly to how this phenomenon occurs to our bodies. Furthermore, they are each tangible and intangible entities. Understanding this deep connection between bodies and landscape supports the argument that the right to landscape is tied to “freedom of expression”.
