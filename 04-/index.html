Intentional Communities for New Social Order

Black | Utopia

The Architects Renewal Committee (ARCH) combined with the Black Power movement in had a pivotal role protecting and preserving the racial and cultural personality of Harlem. It stood against the growing urban renewal and racial integration projects that were sweeping over cities, often dismantling the elements of identity that held the community together. Without the support of social movements or committee’s like ARCH Harlem’s identity could very well be translated to a middle class integrated community with a high probability of pushing the “indigent” members out of the community, those that have historically suffered the most. Or it could be a community slowly falling to urban projects such as a sewer plant or even Colombia’s Gymnasium, drawing a line between Harlem and U. of Columbia. By preserving and emphasizing forms that encompass the “black aesthetic” community activists sought to identify Harlem not “as a liability, but as the mean through which Harlemites could achieve empowerment, self-reliance, and self-determination) (60). Bond, the director of ARCH had a goal to “turn urban renewal projects the the advantage of those typically harmed by it” (61). Bond was a key player nearly destined for this role, and strongly influences project based on him integrating key players primarily African Americans, in critical representative roles within ARCH.

Arcosanti | Utopia

Arcosanti, a experimental urban project put forth in 1970 resisted the growing reliance on highways, and commuting, which inevitably supported the growth of suburbia. Instead, Arcosanti demonstrated the organic nature to which a city can grow and change. In this instance, verticality is at the core of the planning, lending itself to the enjoyment of the surrounding rural landscape for all city dwellers to enjoy, on foot, rather than confined to a vehicle.
