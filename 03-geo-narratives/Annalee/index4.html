In the video “Arcosanti: Paolo Soleri on his futuristic utopian city in the desert,” the values, architecture, and operation of an urban planning experiment are explored. The experiment is a built, standalone settlement based on the premise of abandoning cities, building upward in self-contained agglomerations of human-centered programs, and minimizing environmental impact with climatically-responsive design. The concept at work is archology, which combines architecture and ecology. In this vision, humans dwell in a “habitat that belongs to a landscape.” There are no streets, but an emphasis on self-sustenance through crop cultivation and performative, humanistic, and multi-use architecture. Interestingly, as generations of people move on from the space, incoming generations are left to decide how else the space proceeds; in this way, the planning is democratized. Theoretically, this model of planning minimizes the footprint of human settlement, opting to “build up, not out,” while leaving thousands of acres of unoccupied land in which the natural ecology can flourish uninterrupted.

In Goldstein’s Black Utopia, the author describes how, transcending undignified portrayals, ARCH asserts schema for Harlem in which predominantly Black communities take agency in the planning of local spaces. Emphasizing Black empowerment, the chapter recounts the founding education and entry of a group of Black students into the architectural profession, and how the ARCH group approached its goals of democratized planning, promoting community-forward development (as opposed to that imposed by outsiders), and empowering generations of Black designers. As designers from the community began to reimagine the aesthetic of Harlem, they sought to understand and to establish a Black Aesthetic, with aims to preserve the cultural paving that had been done. They sought new forms and layouts to express the ideas and ways of life that they idealized in the community.

Both readings raise models of urban planning from within (even as the rules for Arcosanti are externally imposed). Furthermore, both raise questions about entitlements to occupy land: in “Arcosanti,” this question is about the space taken up over earth and ecology, while in Black Utopia this pertains to relationships among segregated people and interests.
