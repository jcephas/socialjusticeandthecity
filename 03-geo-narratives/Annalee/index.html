In “Nature and Space” from his book, Seeing Like a State, James C. Scott shows how state-imposed measurements of people, resources, products, and space have historically been created and sanctioned as means to serve the state’s own interests, obscuring “on-the-ground” realities and locally-derived, practical systems. Situated in his larger “case against imperialism of high-modernist, planned social order” and advocacy for “practical knowledge” and “informal processes,” Scott argues that the resilience of social and natural diversities are threatened by obtuse, reductionist measurement sciences introduced by the state. “Certain forms of knowledge and control,” Scott asserts, “require a narrowing of vision.” By homogenizing and abstracting the real practices of local individuals into more legible models of defined coordinates and uniform units of measure, the state achieves greater administrative control. On the other hand, Jeremy Crampton describes in “Critical Cartography” a movement where cartography is democratized by access to information and technological means of generating maps. In this lies a power to generate narratives alternative to those posed by the state or academia. Together, these readings suggest that with more free-flowing information and the tools to assemble and represent that information publicly, the pool of participants in shaping narratives of space, processes, and resources grows. Crampton, especially, focuses on how the movement toward critical cartography has been underpinned by increased use of technology. If elites were able to administer such control by way of parsing land, abstracting realities, and suiting the organization to their needs, it follows that making the practice more public would reissue some of this power to the people once subject to the consequences of those maps. Evidently, it implicates the notion of access. While equipping more of the non-elite public with the means to represent space would make the science of cartography more democratic, we must qualify this potential with questions of access. Suppose the movement illustrated by Crampton continues to accelerate: increasing members of the public take to their computers and make visible the nuanced practicalities of their daily lives, how they use resources or space, or generally express what they wish to make known. The ability to do this rests upon access to that technology. While wide-spread, there remain disparities in who has access to the necessary technology.

In this set of visualizations, A pair of maps created with Tableau Public portray the percentage of people over age 3 who live in a home that successfully accesses the internet, per state. The first is organized without geographic boundaries, and the latter corresponds to US state boundaries. Tableau Public is an organization and application that connects people without formal education in data analytics to easily visualize statistics, fostering accessibility in visualization sciences. A small interactive illustration at right contains a series of rectangles whose widths proportionally represent the racial composition of the population of the United States. Hovering over these rectangles scales each vertically in proportion to the percentage among that group of individuals, above the age of 5, live in a home that accesses the internet. We notice that, not only does internet connectivity vary by state, but it also varies by racial category. We can expect, then, that even positive-sounding democratization of cartography with digital methods is subject to racial disparity. As long as there remain economic and connectivity-related inequities, there will be a continued assertion of a dominant narrative that makes less numerous voices seem even fewer.
